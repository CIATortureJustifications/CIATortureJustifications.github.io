CIA Torture Justifications Post 9/11

Legal Justifications

How the CIA gained legal justifications for EITs from the Department of Justice

After initially justifying EITs Enhanced Interrogation Techniques
 by claiming that traditional rapport building techniques were failing the CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 then needed to gain legal backing to make their actions legal. The very fact the CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 did this infers that they knew the techniques were legally questionable. Director of Central Intelligence, George Tenet, knew that EITs Enhanced Interrogation Techniques
 would cause legal questions to arise as they could constitute torture. Tenet called for direct legal clearence of the techniques before they were used (although Zubaydah had already been subjected to EITs Enhanced Interrogation Techniques
). Tenet signed off on the techniques so long as they were legal, additionally, Tenet lobbied President Bush in order to get further legal support for the CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 and their actions. As a result Bush issued a memorandum on February 7th, 2002, under direction of the DoD Department of Defense
 relating to the legal application of the Geneva Convention Relative to the Treatment of Prisoners of War (1949):

This meant that the treatment of these prisoners were not covered by the Geneva Convention therefore further removing the CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 from any possible legal implications arising from EITs Enhanced Interrogation Techniques
. Note that al-Qaeda and Taliban prisoners are also always refered to as detainees instead of prisoners in order to ensure that along with Bush’s memorandum the Geneva Convention would be even more difficult to apply. This was a significant step towards the giving EITs Enhanced Interrogation Techniques
 legal credibility.

After developing the techniques Mitchell, along with Counterterrrorism Centre ( CTC Counterterrorism Center
) Chief Operating Officer Jose Rodriguez met with George Tenet and Acting General Counsel for the CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 John A. Rizzo to discuss the techniques at length which is where Tenet decided that they needed full legal confirmation:

This is what ultimately triggered the numerous communications between the CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 and DoJ Department of Justice
 which aimed to narrow legal definitions of what constituted torture in attempt to make EITs Enhanced Interrogation Techniques
 legal. Bush’s true knowledge of the program seems unclear as senior members of the CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 stated that Bush was not briefed on the program, however, Bush contradicts this in his book Decision Points where he claimed he was informed. However, this does not detract from the fact that this program still involved very high up members of the CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 and government.

Numerous memorandums were sent between the CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 and DoJ Department of Justice
 , often dubbed as the ‘Torture memos’ however the first was arguably the most significant. This memo was sent from the DoJ Department of Justice
 to White House Counsel Alberto Gonzales (pictured below).

This memo titled Standards of Conduct for Interrogation under 18 U.S.C. §§ 2340–2340A was sent on August 1st, 2002. This memo set out for the first time what would constitute a breach of 2340-2340A thus narrowing the legal definition of torture. It ruled 2340-2340A only covered extreme acts. Stating that ‘severe pain’ covered acts that were hard for a victim to endure and it must be accompanied by severe physical injury, including organ failure and/or death. It also stated that severe mental pain must cause lasting phychological harm like developing a mental disorder and the causes of this suffering are listed in the statute. It further reiterates that techniques the CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 are using may constitute ‘cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment’ but they fail to constitute torture. It also upheld Bush’s memo stating that al-Qaeda and Taliban detainees are not covered by the Geneva Convention. Although there were many more memos to follow this one, it set a presedent to base other legal decisions off of and also was the first to acknoledge that EITs Enhanced Interrogation Techniques
 were within the remits of the law for the first time.

The very same day a memo was issued from the DoJ Department of Justice
 to Rizzo regarding the Interrogation of al-Qaeda operative. This was refering specifically to the interrogation of Abu Zubaydah. It set out the ten approved techniques and established safeguards for each one to further exploit loopholes in the law and protect the CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 and its interrogators from litigation. It further stated that interrogations must also be overseen by medical professionals to determine whether CIA Central Intelligence Agency
 actions were causing severe physical or mental pain or suffering. As interrogations occurred other memos were issued to address other specfics such as who constituted a ‘high-value detainee’, as these detainees were technically the only individuals that could be subjected to EITs Enhanced Interrogation Techniques
. It is stated in one of Steven G. Bradbury’s memos that a ‘high-value detainee’ is an individual that is a senior member of al-Qaeda or a group associated with al-Qaeda, has knowledge of an imminent terror attack or has had involvement in the planning of one and, if released, poses a continued threat.
